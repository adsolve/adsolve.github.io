Event Overview: The Future of Multi-modal Healthcare AI Workshop

On November 13th 2025, AdSoLve hosted a multimodal medical diagnostics & AI workshop bringing together researchers, clinicians, and industry stakeholders to discuss opportunities and challenges for multimodal AI in high-stakes healthcare settings.

The workshop featured presentations from the AdSoLve team, including Professor Maria Liakata (QMUL), Professor Claude Chelala (Barts Cancer Institute), Professor Greg Slabaugh (QMUL), and Iqra Ali (QMUL). The AdSoLve evaluation platform was also demonstrated by Sebastian Löbbers, Cian Kennedy, and Mahmud Akhter, showcasing model-agnostic “evaluation bundles” for radiology report generation that combine semantic metrics, clinical error detection, and human–AI comparison tools. Presentation slides are linked throughout (HERE).

Invited talks were delivered by Professor Hemant Kocher (Professor of Liver and Pancreas Surgery at Barts Cancer Institute, London), who highlighted challenges in pancreatic cancer diagnosis and the importance of high-quality multimodal biobanks for early detection; Dr. Gregory Dean (Chief of Paediatric Urology, St Christophers Hospital for Children, Clinical Professor of Urology, Temple University and Founder of DriQ Health, US), who covered multimodal AI opportunities and safety considerations in paediatric urology; and Professor Philip Resnik (Institute for Advanced Computer Studies and Department of Linguistics, University of Maryland, US), who presented work on multimodal modelling for mental-health assessment, symptom-level insights, and population-relative risk-ranking for suicidality.

A panel discussion on Opportunities and Challenges in Multimodal Medical AI brought together Kathrin Cresswell (Professor of Digital Innovations in Health and Care at the University of Edinburgh), Steven Newhouse (Deputy Chief Information Officer at Barts Health NHS Trust), Chetan Kaher (Chief Innovation Officer, JIVA.AI), Prabhu Arumugam (Clinical Innovation Lead, AWS), and Dan Schofield (AI Technical Specialist, NHS England).

Panelists and breakout groups discussed real-world adoption barriers including workflow integration, infrastructure gaps, data governance and privacy constraints, automation bias, workforce readiness, and the regulatory burden of updating and re-certifying AI-enabled medical software. A recurring theme was that multimodal AI is powerful, but must be bounded by clinical validity and evaluated against practical success metrics such as time saved, reduction of missed findings, clinician burden, and patient experience—not accuracy alone.

Across the day, the closing message was clear: multimodal AI holds exceptional promise for earlier detection, improved diagnostic support, and more personalised care—if developed and evaluated within the realities of clinical practice and NHS deployment constraints.
